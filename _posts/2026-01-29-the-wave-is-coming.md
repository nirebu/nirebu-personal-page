# The Wave Is Coming

I recently read the [AI at work is anti-labor by design](https://henry.codes/writing/ai-at-work-is-anti-labor-by-design/) article. The author argues that AI adoption is fundamentally about eliminating workers, not empowering them. That bosses push LLMs because they're cheaper than your coworker's salary (so they can keep their lifestyles), not because they'll make your work better.

The facts are undeniable. Amazon laying off 14,000. Pinterest cutting 15%. The correlation between "AI adoption" announcements and workforce reductions isn't subtle.

But here's where I diverge: this isn't new behavior wearing a new mask. This is old behavior accelerated.

## Companies Have Always Wanted to Be Lean

A company's purpose is to generate profit. Its secondary purpose is to minimize costs. This has been true since the first merchant hired their first employee. Automation, outsourcing, efficiency initiatives; these aren't AI phenomena. AI is just the latest and most powerful tool in a toolkit that's always existed.

What AI changes isn't the *direction* of travel. It changes the *velocity*.

## The Hard Truth No One Wanted to Hear

AI, by accelerating the rate of making things efficient, is exacerbating hard truths many people didn't want to hear: **some jobs were already close to useless.**

Work that was mostly shuffling information from one format to another. Tasks that existed because humans couldn't process data fast enough. Roles that were really just very expensive search engines.

If a job can be reduced to "take input X, apply known rules, produce output Y" it was always vulnerable since the advent of tools, calculators, computers and commodity software. AI just made the timeline even shorter.

## Memento Mori for Knowledge Workers

I write this from a position of privilege. Right now, I'm not directly threatened. But I hold a constant *memento mori*: this threat will come for me too.

The question isn't "will the wave reach me?" It's "when?" And more importantly: "what will I do when it does?"

## Three Responses to the Wave

Watching colleagues across the industry, I see three distinct responses emerging:

### 1. Stand Ahead of the Wave

Become so expert in so many fields, so connected with the right people, that AI can't touch you. Develop judgment, taste, relationships, and cross-domain expertise that can't be automated.

This is the safest position, but requires continuous investment.

### 2. Ride the Wave

Use AI as a force multiplier. Let it propel you further than you could have gone alone. Accept that riding waves is inherently risky—you might be swept backwards—but embrace the potential to be propelled exponentially forward.

This is my current strategy. Higher variance, higher potential upside.

### 3. Stand Against the Wave

Pretend nothing is happening. Refuse to engage. Declare yourself a Luddite and hope the wave passes.

Waves do pass and bubbles burst. But if this one does so with immense force, it will be even harder to resist.

---

### Breaking the Deadlock

**A question for who staunchly resist:** The [article](https://henry.codes/writing/ai-at-work-is-anti-labor-by-design/) criticizes bosses for wanting to keep their salaries and lifestyles by cutting costs through AI. Fair critique.

But don't employees want the same thing? To keep their salaries, their lifestyles, their current position?

The boss protecting their interests through efficiency gains looks identical to the worker protecting their interests through resistance to change. Both are defending their economic position. Both will cite moral justifications. I'm not saying the power dynamics are equivalent, because they're not. But the underlying motivation is the same: self-preservation.

Here's where it becomes a trap: myopic bosses want to cut employees because they don't see productivity gains. Employees don't provide gains because they resist the tools. The boss sees the resistance as confirming their decision. The employee sees the cuts as confirming their suspicion. Both spiral downward.

But there's an exit: **if workers become force multipliers, there are fewer reasons to cut them.** If you're the person who can do in two hours what used to take a team two weeks (and you do it consistently) you become harder to replace than the tool itself.

Even if the wave trumps you, you have invested in the best asset you have: **YOU**. You've adapted to an ever-changing world instead of pretending it's static. The next endeavor becomes easier, not harder.

If we're honest about self-preservation as the shared motivation, the irony becomes clear: resistance to AI as self-preservation might be the least effective self-preservation strategy available.

## The Real Question

When the wave passes (and it will) where will the pieces be?

Who you know. What you can do. How quickly you adapted. What you built while others were paralyzed.

I don't know yet where I want things to fall. But I know I want to be part of the force deciding it, not watching it happen.

That's worth spending time figuring out before the water settles.

---

*The wave is coming. The only choice is how you meet it.*
